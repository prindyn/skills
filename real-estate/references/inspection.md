# Inspection Phase Navigation

The inspection period is the most emotionally volatile phase of a real-estate transaction. Buyers who were calmly disciplined during the opening now find themselves alternating between panic ("the house is falling apart") and fear ("I'll lose the deal if I ask for too much"). Sellers feel personally attacked when their home is described as having problems.

Your role here: be the calm, numerate voice that treats each finding as a data point rather than a verdict.

## Core frame — this is a recalibration, not a new negotiation

Going in, the buyer agreed to a price based on assumptions about condition. The inspection either confirms those assumptions or updates them. Items that *were reasonably assumed* at the offer price don't justify a credit ("this 1994 house has 1994 finishes" — yes, that was priced in). Items that materially contradict the assumed condition do.

This frame prevents two common failures:

- **Buyer overreach**: treating the inspection as a second auction, asking for credits on cosmetic or age-appropriate items and blowing up the deal over a reasonable number.
- **Buyer caving**: swallowing a major safety or structural issue because "the seller will be mad."

## 1. Safety vs. Cosmetic filter

Categorize every finding into one of four buckets. Different buckets deserve different treatment:

| Bucket | Examples | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| **Critical Safety** | Active gas leak, exposed wiring, failed carbon monoxide sources, structural instability, active roof leak, mold, lead paint in likely-disturbed surfaces, non-functional smoke/CO detectors | Must be addressed or the deal doesn't proceed. Ask for repair or credit. If seller refuses both, walk. |
| **Major Structural / System** | Aging roof near end of life, failing HVAC, foundation cracks of concern, old electrical panel (Federal Pacific, Zinsco), galvanized plumbing | Negotiable. Appropriate credit usually = remaining-life-adjusted replacement cost, not full replacement cost. |
| **Functional System** | Water heater near end of life but working, outdated but functional HVAC, minor plumbing leaks, windows with failed seals | Negotiable but proportional. Bundle into a single reasonable ask. |
| **Cosmetic / Maintenance** | Paint scuffs, carpet wear, old appliances, landscaping, minor caulking, small drywall cracks, fogged glass | Do not negotiate. Age-appropriate for the house's price tier. Expect maintenance. |

Rough rule: **total requested credits should land around 1–3% of purchase price** for a normal inspection, higher only for genuinely distressed or misrepresented properties. Requests much larger than that trigger seller anger and agent pushback regardless of how well-justified each individual line item is.

## 2. Repair credit vs. repair request — which to ask for

| Factor | Favors Repair Credit | Favors Seller Repair |
|---|---|---|
| Buyer will live in house and cares about quality | ✓ Credit lets buyer hire their own contractor | |
| Timeline is tight | | ✓ Repair may be faster than credit negotiation back-and-forth |
| Seller is handy / a contractor themselves | | ✓ They can do it cheaper and well |
| Seller is disengaged (estate, remote, investor) | ✓ They don't want to manage contractors | |
| Item is specialized (HVAC, roof, structural) | ✓ Buyer wants licensed pro with warranty to them | |
| Item has warranty implications | ✓ Credit lets buyer hold warranty direct | |
| Lender will allow it | ✓ (but check — some loan programs limit credits) | |

Default recommendation: **credit, except when a specialty repair truly needs the seller's existing contractor relationship**. Credits avoid "seller's cousin patched it with duct tape" outcomes and give the buyer a post-close dollar buffer for other discoveries.

How to frame a credit request to a seller (they often emotionally prefer "I'll fix it" because it feels like less money):

> "To simplify closing and avoid asking you to manage contractors during your move, we'd prefer a credit of $X at closing. That way you're done at settlement, and we handle the coordination on our side."

The seller comparison that matters: credit = dollars off their proceeds; repair = dollars + their time + warranty follow-up with the buyer. Reframing as convenience usually wins.

## 3. Inspection negotiation cap — bundling and framing

If the inspection finds 20 minor items, listing all 20 looks petty and invites line-by-line refusal. Bundle strategically.

Recommended structure for the credit request:

1. **1–2 Major items** called out by name with cost justification.
2. **One bundled line** for minor items: "Bundle of miscellaneous items per inspection report: $X total." This bypasses nit-picking.
3. **Threshold language**: "We've focused only on safety and functional items over $500 per line; we've absorbed all cosmetic and minor wear items."

This signals to the seller: the buyer is being selective and reasonable. It also gives the seller an emotionally workable story — they're not caving to 20 demands, they're resolving two significant items.

Total-request framing: express it both as a dollar figure and as a percentage of purchase price. "$7,500, or about 1.7% of the purchase price" sounds less aggressive than "$7,500" alone.

## 4. Aging-system negotiation

Systems near end-of-life present a specific challenge: they work *today*, but the buyer will replace them within a predictable window. The seller's response is usually "but it's working fine" — which is true but incomplete.

Depreciation-based framework. Ask for a *prorated* credit based on remaining useful life, not full replacement:

**Credit = Replacement Cost × (1 − Remaining Life / Typical Life)**

| System | Typical life | Typical replacement cost |
|---|---|---|
| Asphalt shingle roof | 20–25 yrs | $12,000–$25,000 depending on size/pitch |
| Architectural shingle roof | 25–30 yrs | $15,000–$30,000 |
| Central A/C | 12–15 yrs | $5,000–$9,000 |
| Gas furnace | 15–20 yrs | $4,000–$8,000 |
| Heat pump | 12–15 yrs | $6,000–$11,000 |
| Water heater (tank) | 10–15 yrs | $1,500–$3,000 |
| Water heater (tankless) | 15–20 yrs | $3,500–$6,000 |
| Septic system | 20–30 yrs | $8,000–$25,000 |

(These ranges shift regionally — confirm with a local contractor.)

Example: HVAC that's 13 years old, typical life 15 years, replacement cost $7,500. Remaining life ~13%. Credit = $7,500 × (1 − 0.13) ≈ $6,525. Reasonable ask: $5,000–$6,500.

Frame this as **"future value adjustment,"** not a current defect:

> "The HVAC is functional now, and we're not characterizing it otherwise. We're adjusting the purchase price for the fact that its useful life ends well before ours as owners begins — effectively, we're paying for a property that will need a $7,500 capital replacement within 2–3 years, and we'd like that recognized in the price."

This language preempts the seller's "but it's fine" response by explicitly conceding current function.

## 5. Emotional dip — proceeding, renegotiating, or walking

Many buyers experience acute doubt during inspection phase even when no findings objectively justify it. Separate the chemistry from the facts.

Decision framework — walk through these with the user in order:

**Question 1: Did the inspection meaningfully violate the assumptions behind my offer?**

- If the house turned out to be a foot-for-foot match with what was expected, findings don't justify renegotiating, let alone walking. Any remorse is emotional.
- If the inspection revealed genuinely new information (hidden water damage, structural issues, undisclosed past work), a recalibration is justified.

**Question 2: Does the recalibrated price (original price minus defensible credits) still fit under my Hard Stop?**

- If yes → proceed, ask for the credits, plan to close.
- If no → the deal has broken. Either get a credit that restores the math, or walk. Don't close at a price that violates the Hard Stop.

**Question 3: Are the fundamentals I originally wanted still present?**

- Location, size, school district, floor plan, neighborhood feel — these rarely change between offer and inspection. If they were right then, they're right now.
- If they've changed (new information about traffic noise, future development, HOA dysfunction), that's legitimately new information.

**Question 4: Is this discomfort about this house, or about any house?**

- If the buyer would feel the same panic about *any* property at this stage, it's generalized anxiety, not property-specific. Acknowledge it, sleep on it, but don't let it drive action.

**Recommendation template for the user:**

> Given the specific findings and the math, here's my recommendation: **[Proceed / Renegotiate for $X / Walk]**, because **[one-sentence rationale tied to Hard Stop or fundamentals]**. If you feel differently after a night's sleep, that feeling itself is useful information — but don't act on it until you've checked it against these numbers in the morning.

## 6. Seller perspective — defending inspection requests

If the user is the seller and a buyer's inspection request has come in:

Principles:

- Treat it as a negotiation input, not a personal attack. The buyer's agent built the list; most sellers' initial "how dare they" reaction is unhelpful.
- Focus responses on *safety and functional* items; pushback on *cosmetic* items is almost always defensible.
- Counter with repair (not credit) on items where you have a reliable contractor and the cost difference is meaningful.
- Compute the compound cost of the credit against your next step (see `seller-defense.md`) — a $5,000 credit shrinks your down payment on your next home by the same amount.

Sample seller counter:

> "We've reviewed the inspection report carefully. We're prepared to address [items A, B] as they represent genuine safety/functional concerns. Items [C, D, E] reflect age-appropriate maintenance that we believe were reasonably factored into the original offer. We propose $X total in credit to cover A and B, which reflects contractor quotes we've obtained."

Note the construction: acknowledge the legitimate items rather than refusing the whole list. A seller who says "no" to everything comes across as unreasonable; a seller who says "yes to these, no to those, here's why" retains credibility.
