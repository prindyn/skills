# Investor-Specific Strategies

Investor decisions differ from owner-occupant decisions in one critical way: they're decided by math, not by how the kitchen feels. Investors who let emotion creep in bleed margin, slowly at first, then all at once on a bad flip.

This reference assumes the user is evaluating rental or flip opportunities with an eye to returns, not lifestyle.

## 1. The 1% Rule — quick filter for rentals

**Monthly rent ≥ 1% of purchase price → passes.**

Purpose: a *filter*, not an oracle. It tells the user whether a property is worth deeper underwriting. Failing doesn't always kill a deal; passing doesn't guarantee it works.

How to use:

- **Passes easily (>1.2%)**: likely cash-flow-positive; underwrite in detail, probably worth an offer.
- **Passes marginally (1.0–1.2%)**: marginal cash flow; sensitive to vacancy, rate, and maintenance. Underwrite before committing.
- **Fails but close (0.7–1.0%)**: increasingly common in high-appreciation markets (CA, Pacific NW, NYC metro, some Sun Belt cities). Deal may still work if appreciation is reliable, but this is speculation, not cash-flow investing. Flag to the user.
- **Fails decisively (<0.7%)**: don't buy for cash flow. Only consider as an appreciation play with a clear thesis and the capital to absorb negative cash flow.

Quick math of the price that *would* make the property pass: `passing price = monthly rent × 100`. This is the buyer's notional ceiling for 1% compliance. Gap between passing price and list price tells you how unrealistic the listing is for a rental buyer.

## 2. The 70% Rule — max offer for flips

**Maximum Allowable Offer = (0.70 × ARV) − repair costs.**

The 30% spread is not profit — it covers:

- Acquisition costs (transfer tax, title, lender fees): ~2%
- Holding costs during rehab and marketing (taxes, insurance, utilities, loan interest for 3–9 months): ~5–10%
- Selling costs (agent commission, title, seller concessions): ~7–8%
- Flipper's profit margin: ~10–15%

When to tighten the rule:

| Condition | Use |
|---|---|
| Softening market, rising rates, uncertain ARV | 65% |
| Long expected hold time (>6 months to resell) | 65% |
| Unfamiliar rehab scope (permit-heavy, structural) | 60–65% |
| Very hot market with short hold times | 72–75% (carefully) |
| Turnkey flip (just cosmetic) | 75% (carefully) |

Inputs the user usually gets wrong:

- **ARV** — almost always optimistic. Use 5+ sold (not listed) comps in the last 90 days, same post-rehab condition. If the user's ARV comes from Zillow's Zestimate or an agent's "conservative estimate," test it hard.
- **Repair estimate** — add 20% buffer. Always run the sensitivity case. Flips that barely work at expected repairs routinely lose money when actual repairs come in high.
- **Timeline** — the repair takes 2× the contractor's estimate, resale takes 1.5× the expected days on market. Plan holding costs accordingly.

Produce for the user:

1. Max Allowable Offer at both 70% and 65%
2. Expected profit at those prices
3. Break-even purchase price
4. Break-even ARV at the current offer
5. Margin under +20% repair cost scenario
6. Whether to make an offer, pass, or wait for price reduction

## 3. Pipeline mentality — comparing deals

The single highest-leverage change a new investor can make is evaluating 10+ deals in parallel rather than one at a time. It removes the emotional attachment that destroys discipline and reveals which deals are actually competitive.

Evaluation grid per deal:

| Criterion | Weight (typical) |
|---|---|
| Cash-on-cash return | High |
| Cap rate (for rentals) | Medium-High |
| Expected annual appreciation | Medium |
| Risk of cost overrun | High |
| Exit flexibility (resale liquidity, rental demand if flip fails) | Medium |
| Renovation complexity and permit risk | Medium |
| Fit with user's operational capacity | High |
| Tenant demand / rental comps | High (rentals only) |

Rank deals. The top 1–2 deserve aggressive, well-researched offers. Deals 3–5 are backup or low-ball targets. Everything below, archive.

The pipeline also changes how aggressively to negotiate any single deal: when three comparable deals are in the funnel, any one deal's collapse is cheap, so the user can hold firm without panic.

Specific effect: when the listing agent says "there are other offers," the investor with a pipeline can truthfully reply "that's fine; we have other deals we're also evaluating" — and mean it. That single sentence, when true, recalibrates the negotiation.

## 4. Distressed property strategy

Distress types and their negotiation signatures:

| Distress type | Primary motivation | Secondary concerns | Offer structure that wins |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-foreclosure | Avoid credit damage, get cash to relocate | Timeline certainty | Fast close (14–21 days), cash or strong pre-approval, enough cash for them to move |
| Estate / inheritance | Convert asset to cash, minimize hassle | Equal distribution among heirs | Clean "as-is" offer, flexible timing if heirs need consensus, no emotional attachment to pre-close negotiations |
| Divorce | Finalize and move on | Emotional neutrality of the transaction | Professional tone, agent-to-agent only, fair price, minimal drama |
| Tired landlord | Stop dealing with tenants / repairs | Tax implications (capital gains, depreciation recapture) | Sometimes seller financing with installment sale helps with taxes; understand their accountant's view |
| Relocation | Close by a date certain | Coordination with new home | Flexible rent-back, fast but not rushed close |
| Contractor/renovator | Unload a stuck project | Save face on a failed venture | Non-judgmental tone, willingness to see flaws, reasonable number |

Principles for engaging distressed sellers:

- Solve their problem first, then focus on price. A distressed seller's *timeline certainty* is often worth more to them than the last $5,000 of price.
- Do not exploit. Exploitative deals close but poison reputation; exploitative agents don't bring repeat deal flow. Treat the seller as someone in a hard moment, not as a mark.
- Expect less competition — most retail buyers won't touch these properties. That's where your margin comes from.

Language for approaching distressed situations:

> "We work with a lot of sellers in transition, and we try to make the process as simple as possible for everyone. We're not the highest-priced offer you'll see, but we're often the most certain to close and the easiest to work with. If that's useful to you, let's talk about what a transaction would look like."

## 5. Reputation as deal flow

Year 1 investors think in terms of individual deals. Year 5 investors think in terms of agent relationships that bring off-market opportunities.

Behaviors that build reputation with agents:

- **Close what you offer on.** Back-out rates below 5% build trust; above 20% gets the investor blacklisted quickly.
- **Move fast and predictably.** Pre-approved or cash, ready to close in 14–21 days, predictable communication.
- **Don't re-trade at inspection** (don't make a clean offer and then demand a huge credit later). The agent community talks, and this is the #1 reputation killer.
- **Be professional on the phone.** Calm, numbers-driven, not pushy. Agents would rather tell a reasonable investor about their next off-market pocket listing than an aggressive one.
- **Refer business back.** Recommend good agents to friends; send off-market leads the agent can't take to other agents; thank-you notes after closings.
- **Close promptly on your word.** If you said 18 days, close on day 18, not day 22.
- **Treat closing-date credits with generosity.** Picking up a $150 per-diem interest cost is trivial and cements a "good to work with" reputation.

Behaviors that destroy reputation:

- Making 10 offers a week with no close rate
- Lowballing aggressively without justification
- Backing out after inspection for minor items
- Contacting the seller directly behind the listing agent
- Public or agent-group complaints about minor issues
- Inspection renegotiation that feels opportunistic

12-month reputation plan:

1. **Months 1–2**: Establish with 3–4 agents. Make 2–3 clean offers. Close on 1. Follow up with thank-you note.
2. **Months 3–6**: Close 2–3 more deals at predictable pace. Ask each agent for other agents they respect. Never ghost an agent.
3. **Months 7–12**: Start receiving off-market calls. These are the deals that make the business work — 20–30% below retail with no competition. Protect this channel by continuing the discipline above.

## 6. Common investor mistakes

- **Overpaying because "it's only $5,000 more"**: $5,000 at 70% rule is $7,142 of lost margin. The 30% compounds in the wrong direction too.
- **Underestimating vacancy and maintenance**: budget 15–25% of rent for vacancy + ongoing maintenance combined; more for older properties. Pro forma numbers that assume 95% occupancy and minimal maintenance are fiction.
- **Assuming appreciation**: cash flow is what pays bills; appreciation is a bonus. Building an investment thesis on appreciation in year 1 is speculation.
- **Emotional attachment to a property**: the moment the user starts picturing themselves in it, they've stopped being an investor. Pattern interrupt: walk out and refuse to walk back in until the math is recomputed.
- **Financing decisions made for aesthetics**: investor loans at 6.5% are ugly but they work. Conventional loans at 7.25% because "I got prequalified" may work worse. Run both.
- **Ignoring local regulations**: rent control, short-term-rental bans, inspection requirements, ADU rules — these are mood-killers that arrive after close. Check before offer, not after.

## 7. When retail-buyer frameworks apply

Investors still use everything in the other reference files for tactical purposes: inspection framework, deadlock breaking, anchoring with precision numbers. The difference is the *ceiling* — investors have a calculated max offer and genuinely won't cross it, because the math is the whole point. An owner-occupant can sometimes justify paying a little over "value" for a home they'll love for 20 years. An investor can't, and shouldn't.
