# Market Intelligence & Research

Data is ammunition. Use this phase to convert the user's vague sense of the market ("it seems hot") into specific facts that justify their offer or listing price and expose the other side's motivation.

Four questions this phase must answer:

1. What is the property actually worth?
2. Is the market appreciating, stable, or cooling?
3. Why is the seller selling (or why isn't the buyer pulling the trigger)?
4. Is the other side's pressure (multiple offers, "best and final" deadline) real or manufactured?

## 1. Comparable sales analysis (comps)

A credible comp set has 5–7 properties, all meeting:

- **Same neighborhood** — ideally within 0.5 miles, same school zone, same side of major dividers (highways, rail, rivers).
- **Sold in the last 90 days** — older sales need time adjustments and open arguments.
- **Within ±15% of subject square footage** — layout matters more than lot square footage in most submarkets.
- **Similar bed/bath count and configuration**.
- **Similar condition** — a "comparable" that was fully renovated is not comparable to an untouched 1998 kitchen.

Adjustments to apply when comps aren't exact:

| Feature | Typical adjustment |
|---|---|
| Sq ft difference | $100–$300 per sq ft depending on local price level |
| Bedroom difference | $10,000–$25,000 per bedroom |
| Bathroom difference | $5,000–$15,000 per bath |
| Garage (1-car) | $5,000–$15,000 |
| Pool | $10,000–$30,000 (can be negative in cold climates) |
| Major kitchen reno (within ~5 yrs) | +$15,000–$40,000 |
| Finished basement | varies; usually $40–$60 per sq ft finished |
| Major system at end of life (roof, HVAC) | − estimated replacement cost |

Adjust comps to the subject, not the subject to comps: if a comp has a pool and the subject doesn't, subtract the pool value from the comp's sale price to get an adjusted value for comparison.

Output for the user:

- Adjusted price range from the comp set
- Midpoint as "justified value"
- Specific dollar-adjusted rationale for the dollar gap between justified value and list price
- **Script for offer letter**: "Based on sales at [addresses] in the last 90 days, adjusted for [the specific differences], the indicated value is $X. Our offer of $Y reflects that analysis."

## 2. Neighborhood trend analysis

The same property justifies very different offer strategies depending on whether the market is warming or cooling. Inputs:

- Average days on market (DOM) now vs. 6 months ago
- Sale-to-list price ratio — currently, 6 months ago
- Active inventory now vs. 3 months ago (supply)
- Recent construction or pipeline development (future supply)
- Employment or population shifts affecting demand

Interpretation:

- **DOM rising + inventory rising + sale/list ratio falling** → cooling/buyer's market. Offer aggressively below list; negotiate hard on inspection; seller is usually anxious.
- **DOM falling + inventory falling + sale/list ratio ≥ 100%** → heating/seller's market. Expect escalation clauses; differentiate on terms; be prepared to pay over asking and bring strong pre-approval.
- **Mixed signals** → balanced market. Normal negotiation dynamics; list price roughly reflects value.

Recommend aggressiveness on a three-point scale: conservative (close to list, few contingencies stripped), moderate (a few percent off list, standard contingencies), aggressive (meaningfully below list and/or with strong contingencies intact).

## 3. Days on market strategy

DOM is a leakage signal. A property at 2× the neighborhood's average DOM has a seller who is either overpriced or emotionally attached to a number they can't defend.

Framework:

- **DOM ≤ neighborhood average**: seller expects close-to-asking. Don't lowball dramatically; you'll be dismissed.
- **DOM = 1–2× average**: seller is getting nervous. Expect 3–7% discount off current list is defensible.
- **DOM = 2–3× average**: seller is in trouble. 5–12% off current list is defensible; a price reduction is coming if an offer doesn't arrive.
- **DOM > 3× average, multiple price reductions**: seller is stuck. Look for structural reasons (bad layout, deferred maintenance, noisy street) that might also limit your eventual resale value.

If relisted: MLS relist tricks (cancel + re-enter) sometimes reset DOM artificially. Check listing history in public records or Zillow's timeline to spot this — it changes the real DOM and the real seller motivation.

Language for the offer letter (referencing DOM without insulting the seller): "Given the property's time on market and the recent absorption rate in [neighborhood], we're structuring a competitive offer that reflects current market absorption rather than list price."

## 4. Seller motivation detective

Motivation is usually the most valuable piece of information and the least directly observable. Assemble it from signals.

Observable signals:

- **Vacant home** → often high motivation (carrying costs hurt)
- **Estate sale or trust sale** → heirs usually want cash, not a protracted negotiation
- **Relocation (out-of-state address on tax records)** → motivated to close by a date
- **Recent divorce / death records** → sometimes motivated, sometimes not (grief can extend, not shorten, timelines)
- **Seller already has new-construction elsewhere under contract** → deadline-driven, motivated
- **Listing language "must sell," "all offers considered," "bring offers"** → high motivation openly signaled
- **Listing language "firm," "no lowballs," "serious buyers only"** → low motivation or prior frustration

Questions to have the buyer's agent ask the listing agent (indirectly — these should sound conversational, not interrogative):

1. "How long has the seller owned the home?"
2. "Have they already purchased something else?"
3. "Is the seller local, or is this a remote sale?"
4. "How much flexibility do they have on the closing date?"
5. "Have there been any offers that fell through, and why?"
6. "Is there a reason they're selling now rather than last year or next year?"
7. "Would the seller consider a rent-back arrangement?"
8. "Is the seller open to a credit in lieu of specific repairs?"
9. "Are there any specific contingencies that concern them?"
10. "Anything non-obvious about the house we should know about?"

Interpret *how* the listing agent answers, not just what they say. Hesitations, overly crisp "that's confidential" answers, and eager information-sharing all carry signal.

Public records worth checking: purchase date and price, any permit history, tax records, any liens or judgments, MLS listing history.

## 5. Multiple offer verification

When the agent says "we have multiple offers, please submit your best and final by [deadline]," treat it as a claim to be tested, not a fact to be obeyed.

Probability estimator — a multiple offer claim is more likely real when:

- DOM is short (≤ neighborhood average)
- Market is hot (sale/list ≥ 100%)
- The home is priced below or at apparent market value
- The listing agent agrees to put the claim in writing
- The deadline is 24–48 hours out, not 4 hours out

Less likely real when:

- DOM is long, with previous reductions
- Listing agent is vague about details (how many offers, cash vs. financed, when each came in)
- Deadline is extremely tight
- The listing agent has a reputation for using the tactic
- The property has been relisted after a prior failed sale

How to verify without revealing weakness:

- Ask the agent in writing: "Can you confirm in writing that multiple offers are currently in hand?" Some agents will decline or quietly drop the claim.
- Ask: "Would the seller accept an offer today at $X without the escalation or best-and-final process?" A real multiple-offer situation won't short-circuit; a manufactured one often will.
- Ask the listing agent to disclose whether other offers are financed or cash, and whether the competing offers are contingent on home sale — neutrally phrased, this is a normal request and the response is informative.

If real: escalate thoughtfully — pay up to value, not to win; strengthen terms (larger earnest money, shorter contingencies, faster close) rather than just increasing price. See `opening-offer.md` for escalation-clause construction.

If manufactured: hold your position and respond on a reasonable timeline. Often the "other offers" evaporate when you don't flinch.

Script when holding firm under pressure:

> "We're committed to the $X offer we submitted on [date]. That reflects our analysis of comparable sales and the property's condition. If there's a stronger offer that meets the seller's needs, we understand, and we'd welcome being informed if the property becomes available again."

This respects the other side, doesn't impugn their honesty, and leaves the door open — which is exactly the door that reopens a week later when the phantom bidder disappears.
