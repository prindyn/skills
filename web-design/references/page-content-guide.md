# Page Content Guide

What each standard page's job is, what content belongs on it, and what to leave out. Read whichever section matches the page the user is currently working on.

## Home page

**Job:** Make a clear first impression. In the first 5 seconds, the visitor needs to know:
1. Who you are
2. What you offer
3. What to do next

**Sometimes called:** Splash page, Landing page (though "Landing Page" usually refers to a one-page site for lead generation specifically — see the *Landing page* note below).

### Required elements

- **Hero:** headline + subhead + primary CTA + visual. The hero does most of the work.
- **What you offer:** 2–4 sections introducing your services or products with benefits, not specs.
- **Proof:** at least one testimonial, client logo, or stat.
- **Final CTA:** restate the primary action at the bottom of the page.

### Strong recommendations

- A short video on the homepage substantially increases time on page and conversion. Even a 30-second loop of you/your work helps.
- Apply the **StoryBrand framework** (Donald Miller): the visitor is the hero, you are the guide. Your homepage walks them through their problem → your plan → success. Avoid making the homepage a monologue about you.
- Keep copy concise. Visitors scan. Long paragraphs lose them.

### What NOT to do

- Don't list every service in the hero. The hero needs *one* clear message.
- Don't lead with a generic value proposition ("We provide quality solutions"). Be specific or be skipped.
- Don't include a stock-photo handshake. It's a meme of bad web design at this point.

## Services / Products page

**Job:** Detail what you offer in a way that mirrors the actual real-world offer. The visitor should leave knowing exactly what they'd be buying and at roughly what price.

### Required elements

- **One section per service/product.** Same internal structure each time: name → who it's for → what it includes → outcome → CTA.
- **Pricing or pricing context.** If you don't show prices, say *why* ("every project is scoped individually") so it doesn't feel hidden. Hidden pricing erodes trust.
- **Proof.** Testimonials, case studies, client logos. Build credibility specific to *that* service.
- **Clear CTA per service.** Different services may warrant different CTAs ("Book a call" vs "Buy now").

### Common mistakes

- Conflating features (what it is) with benefits (what it does for the visitor). Lead with benefits.
- One giant wall of text describing all services. Break it up.
- Using your internal jargon. Use the words your customers use.

## About page

**Job:** Build trust. Tell the story, introduce the people, share the mission. Your opportunity to make the visitor feel a connection to a real human or team.

### Required elements

- **The "why."** What problem made you start this? Lead with that, not your resume.
- **Photo of the human(s).** A real photo. Not a logo. Not a silhouette.
- **Short bio.** Under 250 words per person. Keep the multi-page CV elsewhere.
- **Mission/values, briefly.** Specific, not generic ("We believe in quality" is meaningless).
- **Optional: press mentions, awards, "as featured in" logos.** Build credibility.
- **CTA.** Most often "Get in touch" or "See our work." Don't leave the page without one.

### Tone

- Authentic. The About page is the most "you" page on the site. If you're warm, be warm. If you're technical, be technical. Don't perform a personality.
- Specific over general. "I started Gentle Media after watching small business owners pay agencies $20K for sites that didn't work" beats "I'm passionate about helping businesses succeed."

## Blog / News / Resources page

**Job:** Ongoing engagement, SEO (new keywords on the site), and demonstrating expertise.

### Required elements

- **Index page** listing posts: title, short excerpt, date, category, image.
- **Individual post pages.** Each with H1 = title, scannable subheads, short paragraphs.
- **Topic pillars.** Choose 3–5 themes the blog covers. Without focus, you spray and miss.
- **Posting cadence.** Plan it before launching the blog. Inconsistency is more damaging than infrequency. Once a month, kept to, beats "weekly" that drops off after 3 weeks.

### Content principles

- Each post starts by telling the reader what they'll learn and why it matters to them.
- End with a CTA related to the post's topic, not a generic homepage push.
- Use real examples and specifics. Generic advice ranks poorly and reads worse.

### SEO basics

- Title tag and meta description per post.
- Use the keyword you're targeting in the H1, the URL slug, and the first paragraph — naturally, not stuffed.
- Internal links to related posts and relevant service pages.
- Alt text on every image.

## Contact page

**Job:** Make it trivial to get in touch. Nothing else.

### Required elements

- **Contact form** with the minimum fields needed. Name, email, message is usually enough. Every additional field reduces submissions measurably.
- **Submit confirmation.** When the form succeeds, say so warmly and tell them when to expect a reply.
- **Backup contact info.** Email address and/or phone, in case the form fails or they prefer.
- **Hours / response time expectation.** "We reply within 1 business day" sets expectations.

### Optional but useful

- **Map / location** if you have a physical address visitors come to.
- **Social links** in the footer.

### What NOT to do

- Don't add 10 form fields. Asking for company size, industry, budget, and timeline before they've ever spoken to you is a wall.
- Don't make the contact form the only way to reach you. Some visitors won't trust forms; let them email directly.
- Don't pile content onto this page. The contact form is the page. Anything else is in the way.

## Landing page (one-page variant)

**Job:** Lead generation or single-conversion focus. One page, one goal, one CTA.

### Structure (top to bottom)

1. **Hero** — headline + subhead + CTA + visual.
2. **Problem** — name the visitor's pain.
3. **Offer** — the solution. What you give them and what they need to do.
4. **Proof** — testimonials, stats, client logos.
5. **How it works** — 3 simple steps.
6. **Objection-handling** — FAQ-style answers to "but what about…" concerns.
7. **Final CTA** — restate the offer and the action.

### When to use a landing page vs. a multi-page site

- Use a landing page when there's *one specific thing* you want every visitor to do (sign up, register, buy a single product, book a call for one specific service).
- Use a multi-page site when you have multiple services, an ongoing content strategy, or your visitors need to evaluate you across multiple dimensions before they're ready to act.
- A business can have both: a multi-page main site, plus dedicated landing pages for specific campaigns.
