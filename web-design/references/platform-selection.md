# Platform Selection — Squarespace vs Wix vs WordPress

The three platforms that dominate the small-to-medium website market. Each fits a different user. The right choice depends primarily on the user's *technical comfort* and *what the site needs to do* — not on which platform is objectively "best."

## Quick decision tree

1. **Will the user write any code, or want to learn?**
   - No → Squarespace or Wix.
   - Yes → WordPress is on the table.
2. **Do they value polished defaults over total control?**
   - Polished defaults → Squarespace.
   - Total control → Wix (no-code) or WordPress (with code).
3. **Is cost the dominant constraint?**
   - Yes → WordPress (self-hosted) is cheapest long-term but has the highest setup cost in time.
   - No → Squarespace pays for itself in time saved.

**Default recommendation (when in doubt): Squarespace.** It's what the source guide recommends, for the same reason — best balance of design quality, ease of use, and all-in-one convenience for non-developers.

## Squarespace

**Best for:** small business owners, creatives, bloggers, anyone who wants a polished, professional site without learning a tool inside-out.

**Pros:**
- **User-friendly:** intuitive drag-and-drop editor with sensible defaults
- **All-in-one:** hosting, domain, SSL, and (on higher plans) e-commerce included
- **Design quality:** templates are modern, mobile-responsive, and SEO-aware out of the box
- **Support:** 24/7 customer support via email and live chat

**Cons:**
- **Limited customization:** fewer third-party integrations than WordPress
- **Pricing:** more expensive than self-hosted WordPress
- **Scalability ceiling:** not the right tool for very large or very complex sites

**Use case fit:** restaurants, consultants, photographers, small e-commerce stores, portfolios, event sites, single-location service businesses.

## Wix

**Best for:** users who want pixel-level drag-and-drop control and don't mind spending more time fine-tuning.

**Pros:**
- **Flexibility:** drag any element anywhere — total layout freedom
- **Ease of use:** beginner-friendly, no coding required
- **App Market:** large catalog of add-ons for added functionality
- **Free plan:** allows full testing before committing to paid plans

**Cons:**
- **Easy to break the design:** the same flexibility that empowers also lets you create cluttered, inconsistent pages
- **Performance:** historically slower load times than Squarespace
- **Template lock-in:** switching templates after launch is painful (often a rebuild)

**Use case fit:** users with a strong visual idea who want to execute it themselves and don't mind tinkering.

## WordPress

**Best for:** developers, technically-comfortable users, anyone who needs unusual functionality, sites that will scale significantly, or anyone where hosting cost matters.

**Pros:**
- **Most flexible platform:** powers >40% of the web; thousands of themes and plugins
- **Open-source / free:** no platform fees (but you pay for hosting, possibly themes/plugins)
- **SEO-friendly:** with the right plugins (Yoast, RankMath), excellent SEO control
- **Scalable:** can grow from a personal blog to enterprise-grade sites

**Cons:**
- **Steeper learning curve:** especially around hosting, themes, plugins, and security
- **Requires maintenance:** updates, backups, and security patches are *your* responsibility
- **Quality varies:** plugins and themes range from excellent to abandoned
- **No bundled hosting:** you choose and pay for hosting separately (Bluehost, SiteGround, Kinsta, etc.)

**Use case fit:** content-heavy sites, blogs at scale, custom membership sites, multi-language sites, or anyone who has a developer involved.

## Cost reality (rough 2025 ballpark)

- **Squarespace:** $16–$49/month depending on plan. Domain ~$20/year (often free first year).
- **Wix:** $17–$59/month. Domain similar to Squarespace.
- **WordPress (self-hosted):** $5–$30/month for hosting + $15/year for domain + $0–$200/year for premium themes/plugins.

## Common mistake

Choosing a platform based on a feature comparison spreadsheet instead of who's going to *use* it day-to-day. The platform the owner can confidently update and maintain beats the "more powerful" platform they'll be afraid to touch.

If the user is paralyzed comparing platforms — recommend Squarespace, give them the one-line "why" (best balance for non-developers), and move on. The cost of a slightly suboptimal platform is far less than the cost of a website that never launches.
