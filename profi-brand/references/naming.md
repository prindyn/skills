# Brand Name Generation

A good brand name is **easy to say**, **easy to spell**, **distinctive**, and **survives** as the business evolves. Most amateur names fail one of these. This file gives you the methodology to produce names that don't.

## What you need from the user before generating

If they haven't already given it, get:

1. **What does the business do?** (one sentence)
2. **Who is it for?** (the customer, not the buyer if those differ)
3. **What's the personality?** (3–5 adjectives — push for specific ones)
4. **Any naming constraints?** (must include a word, must avoid a word, must be one syllable, must not sound like a competitor)
5. **Is this a global play or local?** (affects which languages you screen against)

If the user is impatient, get items 1–3 and move. Items 4–5 are nice-to-have but not required to produce solid candidates.

## The seven naming patterns

Every viable brand name belongs to one of these archetypes. When generating, try at least 4 different patterns rather than producing 12 names that all sound the same — variety lets the user feel the trade-offs.

### 1. Founder/Person name
*Examples: Ford, Hewlett-Packard, Warby Parker, Ben & Jerry's, Patagonia (founder's nickname)*

**Strengths:** Inherent authenticity, easy to trademark, never feels generic.
**Weaknesses:** Says nothing about what you do; harder to sell the business later; locked to one person's reputation.
**When to suggest:** Service businesses, professional firms, founder-driven products where the person *is* the differentiator.

### 2. Descriptive/Compound
*Examples: General Electric, PayPal, Salesforce, Whole Foods, Bank of America*

**Strengths:** Tells you exactly what they do; easy to remember once you know.
**Weaknesses:** Often boring; usually owned (the obvious .com is gone); limits expansion if you outgrow the description.
**When to suggest:** Local businesses where clarity > distinctiveness, or B2B where buyers Google by category.

### 3. Suggestive/Metaphorical
*Examples: Amazon (vast like the river), Tesla (electricity pioneer), Uber (German for "above"), Stripe (a stripe of payment infrastructure)*

**Strengths:** Distinctive yet meaningful, ages well, gives you a story to tell.
**Weaknesses:** Customers don't immediately know what you do — needs marketing to bridge the gap.
**When to suggest:** Tech, consumer brands, anything where the founder wants room to grow into the name.

### 4. Invented/Coined
*Examples: Kodak, Xerox, Häagen-Dazs (sounds Danish, isn't), Spotify, Lyft (changed spelling)*

**Strengths:** Trademarkable, the .com is usually available, no preconceptions.
**Weaknesses:** Costs more to make memorable (people have to learn it); can feel fake if too obviously made-up.
**When to suggest:** Tech/SaaS especially, or any brand that needs a strong trademark moat.

### 5. Acronym/Initialism
*Examples: IBM, AT&T, ESPN, BMW, GAP*

**Strengths:** Concise, easy to say.
**Weaknesses:** Hard to make memorable from cold start (most acronyms are forgettable until they're famous); harder to trademark.
**When to suggest:** Almost never for a new brand. Acronyms work after the full name has earned awareness — they're a contraction, not a starting point. If user insists, push back gently.

### 6. Foreign-language word
*Examples: Volkswagen ("people's car"), Sony (from Latin "sonus"), Audi (Latin "to listen"), Häagen-Dazs (faux-Danish)*

**Strengths:** Distinctive, often available as .com, can encode meaning.
**Weaknesses:** Pronunciation friction in the target market; meaning is invisible to most customers.
**When to suggest:** When the user wants distinctiveness with a hidden meaning, and the foreign word is short enough to not trip up English speakers.

### 7. Allusion/Cultural reference
*Examples: Nike (Greek goddess), Starbucks (Moby-Dick character), Penguin (publishers), Apple (Beatles homage)*

**Strengths:** Carries built-in associations; feels intentional and considered.
**Weaknesses:** The reference might be obscure to your audience or might shift in meaning over time.
**When to suggest:** When the founder has a real story tying the reference to the brand's values — not when it's just "this word sounds cool."

## How to generate

When you produce names for a user, structure your response like this:

1. **Pick 3–4 patterns** that fit their stated personality and stage.
2. **Generate 3–4 candidates per pattern** (so 10–15 total).
3. **For each candidate, write 1–2 sentences** explaining the rationale (why this fits their adjectives) and naming the pattern it belongs to.
4. **Flag obvious risks** — common misspellings, embarrassing translations in major languages, conflicts with well-known brands.
5. **End with a recommendation** — your top 3 picks and *why those three*.

## Validation tests

After generating, run each finalist through these. Either run them yourself in your response, or hand the user the checklist.

- **Phone test.** Say it out loud. Could a stranger spell it correctly after hearing it once? Names that fail the phone test cause permanent friction (you'll spend the rest of your life saying "K-O-D-A-K… that's K, O, D, A, K").
- **Search test.** Google the name in quotes. Are there active businesses with the same name in any industry? Major conflicts kill SEO and risk trademark issues. Minor conflicts (a defunct company, a band, a fictional character) are usually fine.
- **Trademark first-pass.** Search the USPTO TESS database (or the equivalent in their country) for active marks in their class. Don't promise it's clear — that needs a lawyer — but flag obvious conflicts.
- **Domain availability.** Check if the .com exists. Don't kill a name just because the .com is taken — see `domain.md` for alternatives — but factor it into the recommendation.
- **Translation check.** Run the name past Google Translate in 3–4 major languages (especially if the brand might go global). The classic example is Chevy "Nova" supposedly meaning "no go" in Spanish (myth, but the principle holds — a quick check costs nothing).
- **5-year test.** Will this name still feel right when the business has 10× more revenue, a different product line, or international customers? Names that lock you into your starting niche become liabilities.

## Naming traps to call out

When you see the user gravitating toward these, push back:

- **The "we'll just add the industry word" trap.** "Acme Solutions", "TechCorp Industries", "Smith Consulting Group" — these are the most generic possible names. Solutions, Group, Industries, Corp, Co are filler words that signal a name was chosen by committee.
- **The "spell it weird" trap.** Replacing letters with numbers ("Krazy4Coffee"), dropping vowels ("Flickr" worked, but only because of the era — most attempts feel try-hard now), or random capitals ("iThing"). Distinctive spelling works occasionally; deliberate misspelling for distinctiveness alone usually doesn't.
- **The "double meaning that no one will get" trap.** Founders love hidden meanings. Customers don't notice them. If the name doesn't work *without* the explanation, the explanation is doing too much work.
- **The "describes too narrowly" trap.** "Boston Bagels" is fine until you open in Cambridge or stop selling bagels. "Boston Pizza" is a real chain that solved this by selling pizza everywhere — but it's a constant compromise. Avoid baking your starting niche into the name unless you're truly committing to that niche forever.
- **The "sounds like a competitor" trap.** If the user's name is one letter off from a famous brand in their industry, it'll feel derivative even if no trademark is violated. Distinct ≠ different-letter-of-a-known-name.

## Sample generation flow (compressed)

> **User:** "I'm starting a sustainable kids' clothing brand. Adjectives: playful, durable, honest. Customer is millennial parents who care about sourcing."
>
> **You:** Generate ~12 names across 4 patterns, e.g.:
> - **Suggestive:** *Mossfield, Tidepool, Acorn & Ash, Wildling*
> - **Founder/Person feel:** *June & Wren, Otto's, Marigold Co.*
> - **Compound descriptive:** *Honest Threads, Slow Stitch*
> - **Invented:** *Ploom, Gritley, Nimba*
>
> Score each on the rationale. Recommend top 3 with reasoning, flag *Honest Threads* as competing with the Honest Company, and run domain availability for the top 3.

That's the level of specificity to aim for.
